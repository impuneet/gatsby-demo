Album Description

Artist: Frank Turner
Year: 2013
Type: Studio Album
Personal Notes: Tape Deck Heart is the first album of Frank Turner's that I owned. A year after first hearing Frank on NPR's Tiny Desk Concerts and now owning every album and EP, it's honestly the worst work of Frank Turner's, but it was also the most commercially successful, reaching #2 in the UK and #52 in the US. It introduced me to the magic that is Frank Turner, so I can't give it too much flak. As a whole, it doesn't feel as emotional or symphonic as the other albums, but the tracks that are good are extraordinary.

Thoughts:

This was the first song that Frank Turner and Matt Nasir (on the mandolin) played in the NPR Tiny Desk Concert, and honestly, who can resist a good break-up song?

It's energetic while being sentimental. It makes you want to dance while being a break-up song. Although this track isn't as symphonic as Frank Turner's best, there's still a climax that leaves you breathless. It's just a bit too pop to really be the usual acoustic rock symphony.

But, this was the first song I heard, and it had me hooked. I can't say it's my favorite song, but I can't say it's not good. It's great. The lyrics in particular are so memorable and quotable, and this song would probably win if this was a purely lyrical review.

Thoughts:

What's extraordinary about Frank Turner is how each track manages to be a symphony with crescendos and layers on layers while still being so simple. All we have in this song is Frank's voice as melody, the guitar as harmony, and drums to get the beat down.

The lyrics and the meaning of this song are also so simple, Frank isn't trying to impress you with how poetic he is. With Tell Tale Signs, Rich Costey, the producer, made Frank sing it 42 times to get the perfect fatigued, raw tone we now hear.

This song is absolute genius and I often come back to this song feeling extremely emotional. It's now been a year since I first heard this track, but it's a timeless track that's brimming with feelings.

Thoughts

Four Simple Words is the perfect example of a Frank Turner symphony. It starts so innocent, so sweet, and you don't expect it to go where it goes. Then, at 1 minute and 22 seconds, the entire game changes. The intensity ramps up to a level that should've made the transition steep and awkward, but instead, it's smooth and gives me shivers each time I hear it. This song manages to pump me up better than any other Frank Turner song.

This song's lyrics and flow are really congruent with the Frank Turner philosophy, so if anyone wants to get into Frank's music, I'd say start with this song (and this album in general). If you like it, dive backwards. See how far it takes you.
