Imagine you're a native of one of the biggest cities of your whole world, Tenochtitlan. Your nation is prosperous, large, and technologically advanced. Up until Hernán Cortés comes up and ruins the party, that is.

There are three reasons why your home was mercilessly destroyed:

  1. The enemies have superior tech. This is the standard answer that most people think of in regards to the Native Americans. Although the Aztecs were incredibly advanced, they couldn't even invent the wheel.
  2. You were too trusting. Your leader, Montezuma, thought that Hernán was a god, Quetzalcoatl. You showered him with so much gold that he destroyed your entire empire.
  3. You have no resistance to the Spanish diseases. This killed off 90% of natives across the Americas. There was no resistance in your body because you'd never been exposed to it before.

You may still think we're imaging. Guess what? We're not.

You are an Aztec, and there are plenty of Hernán Cortés' out there to try and plunder your gold in exchange for your life.

Welcome to the New-New World, the cyberworld.

I'm perfectly aware that the internet has been around for 20 years+, but it also took Hernán and his crew 25 years after Columbus' landing to pillage the Aztecs.

Just as these 25 years were left for exploration and documentation, these past 20 years were a series of experiments. People put their first products online, people uploaded movies to the web, people are making entire livings off of the internet, and people have created multi-billion dollar companies from the internet.

But now, Hernán Cortés and his crew are taking over. As part of the masses, you're simply waiting to get controlled, scammed, and used for illicit activity.

There are a couple of things that you need to understand before I can properly inform you on how to combat this. If you don't understand why you need to combat this, then you'll continue on your life believing that you're safe.

You're not.

3 Things You Need to Understand About the Web

#1: Everything is permanent.

Let's say you're doing something bad. This could be illegal, or you could just be flirting with your boyfriend's best friend.

If you talked about it on Facebook, even through Private Message--guess what, you're screwed. Deleted your account or deleted the messages? Facebook still has it, and Facebook will give the data to the government or your boyfriend.

Doesn't feel relevant? Let's say you got wasted the night of your high school graduation and someone uploaded a picture of you doing so. Even if you un-tag yourself or ask them to take it down, it's still there, forever.

What if you do nothing bad? You're a goody-goody and your record is extremely clean. None of this is going to harm you.

I want to show you something. You have a Google account, right? I bet your email address ends in @gmail.com and your homepage is google.com. Google, and anyone who knows your password can see every single thing you've ever searched for and every single link you clicked from it.

If that doesn't creep you out, I don't know what will. When I found this out and looked through everything, I found a couple of searches that were made when someone was logged into my account. They were...interesting, in the least.

#2: Closed is bad, open is good.

First of all, what's closed and open on the web?

Closed services are like Apple, Google, Facebook--pretty much everything you use by default.

Most people have iPhones or at least know how one works, correct? When you're trying to download a new song, why aren't you able to directly from a site like Bandcamp or the musician's site? Why do you have to go on a computer, plug in your USB drive, download the song onto the computer, drag it into buggy iTunes, pray it doesn't explode, and wait for it to sync?

Meanwhile, with Firefox OS, you can download whatever you want without the computer.

I never had an iPhone, but for my 14th birthday, I got an iPod Nano. Every time I wanted to download new music, I had to use iTunes, which is such a pain. Luckily, it stopped working after nearly two years (what a waste of $160) and I got the Sandisk Sansa Clip, with at least twice the space for $100 less.

This uses a drag-and-drop interface like plugging in a USB drive, supports virtually all audio file formats, and allows for you to expanded to up to 72GB with the use of a microSD card.

That is open.

Wordpress, the software I'm using to relay this information towards you, is open. The theme I'm using on this website is open. The best media player ever is open.

By coincidence, all of the software I mentioned above are open source (meaning that if you want to see the exact code that went into the application, you can) and they're free, but open doesn't have to mean free.

So yes, if you're a content creator, you can earn money from your content, but it can still be open. (See Sublime Text Editor as an example.)

#3: You're being censored.

More than hardcore porn and terrorist allegations, real people trying to get across real messages of change can get censored very easily.

If you're trying to rally change that Facebook doesn't like, and you're using Facebook to get this message across, Facebook can shut you down whenever they want.

This seems logical, right? Facebook's a company, they can do what they want.

But as an activist, you wonder--how else can I get people's attention if not through Facebook?

Is that what we've come to? Relying on closed systems to relay our messages?

I have a website, which means that I have a much better chance at getting my message across. Wordpress is open, which means it won't stop me from saying whatever I want. The only thing that may stop me is my hosting provider, Siteground, but the chance of that happening is much less than if I was writing this on Facebook. (Note: There are web hosting providers if you are doing something a bit fishy. I only use Siteground because it's cheap.)

Even if you're not trying to change the world, you could get censored for just about anything. If you've ever been banned from a forum (throwback to the early 2000's), you know what I mean.

If you want more information on this, EFF is great at explaining things.

15 Steps to Regaining Your Rights Online

Let me clear something up, before you start to doubt me again. I am not a tech guru. I know HTML, I know how to torrent, and I know that viruses don't cause all of computer's problems. Everything I'm about to advise below is coming from a fellow not-too-techy person. You can be assured that everything is pretty easy to do. If you have any troubles, email me at hi@rmorabia.com and I'll see what I can do to help.

The list below goes in steps from easiest to hardest. Don't do everything at once. Some steps will take a long time to complete. I still haven't finished all 15. Once you do, however, you'll be a lot safer than the masses, and you can move on to stuff like HideMyAss and Hyperboria. Ev Bogue's got you covered after this.

#1: Stop searching with Google.

DuckDuckGo, which is an alternative to Google, has some fancy websites that illustrate the issue with Google/Bing/Yahoo! with more eloquence than I possess:

  • DontBubble.Us - all about the creepy filter bubble that keeps you biased.
  • DontTrack.Us - see the web history issue above.

There are alternatives, and yes, they are as good as Google Search.

DuckDuckGo is based off of Yahoo!, and is the most popular alternative. It has a nice UI and allows for the fancy shortcut searches that Google has.

If you need, need, need the Google algorithms, Startpage has you covered. It's based off of Google, but has none of the creepy privacy issues.

This is the easiest step out of the 15. Both sites offer easy ways to add them to your browser, and you should be set. Not using Google Search is the easiest way to protect yourself, trust me.

#2: Get off of Gmail.

Although Gmail has a nice UI, it is not safe, at all. Like the above Facebook example, Google can choose to not let your emails go through, or not let people's emails reach you.

Second, the service isn't as free as you think. Ads are littered everywhere and Google reads your emails to tailor the ads accordingly.

There's an easier, ad-free option, Hushmail. As you can probably tell, I use it myself. It's not totally safe for privacy, but it's the easiest and most reliable option that doesn't require massive tech skills. If you're sending anything you only want one person to read, there's a super easy encryption option embedded in the UI.

You'll notice that you only get 25MB free, which some people might find appalling. I've been under that constraint for a few months now without trouble. If you need extra space and want to support a company doing good things (think of the 25MB as a trial), pay $50 a year.

Google's coaxed us all into believing everything should be free. When you're not the customer, you're the product. Pay for your stuff if you get a regular paycheck.

#5: Install Adblock, Disconnect, HTTPS Everywhere, and NoScript.

These are a couple of safety add-ons for Firefox. For Tor, you don't need to download any add-ons.

  • Adblock Plus - as the name suggests, it blocks ads. It does, however, display ethical ads, such as Project Wonderful and Reddit.
  • Disconnect - requests come in all the time from advertisers, trackers, and social networks. Disconnect blocks you from these.
  • HTTPS Everywhere - this add-on attempts to make all connections secure, if the site allows it. Otherwise, when you're inputting information, it's easier for hackers to get it.
  • NoScript - this acts as alongside Disconnect, and it blocks scripts. I recommend turning off scripts globally, and turning them on temporarily if needed. When using NoScript, the most secure sites will come up with only one request--the domain name. Mine comes up with two. I'm working on that.

All of these add-ons will increase your privacy and safety on the web, though at the same time, they'll probably make your browsing experience faster. It's a win-win.

If RSS is too technical for you, most sites now have email lists. I do, too. If, however, your favorite site doesn't have an email newsletter, you can use an RSS to email service.

#13: Use IRC.

IRC is an open chatting service. Better than iMessage, better than AIM, better than Facebook Mesages, IRC doesn't save a thing unless you're in a channel that specifically says they log (eg. support channels for various softwares).

Right now, getting on IRC is easy. You can go to kiwiirc.com and log into any channel you want. Invite your friends to that channel, and all they have to do is enter in their nickname and the channel you designated.

The cool thing about IRC is that you can use it anywhere. It's open. If you don't want to use Kiwi's web client, download Pidgin for the desktop or various clients for your mobile device. (The server for Kiwi is irc.rizon.net.)

It's much easier to get your friends on when they don't have to sign up.

Note: If you use Skype, stop that, too. Skype is easily tracked and you are not safe on it.

#15: Read the Terms and Conditions/Privacy Policy of every service you use.

You have no clue what Facebook, Google, etc., are taking from you when you sign up, do you?

They could be asking you for slave labor and you couldn't know it, because you checked the box without reading the terms and conditions.

Every time you sign up for a new service, try with all of your might to read the TaC/PP. If you don't want to read it, don't sign up. It's that simple.

There's a cool site that's coming up that offers to make the Terms of Service easier to read. It's called TOS;DR. Right now, the offerings are limited, but it'll definitely help you decide with Facebook.

BONUS #1: Check the monetization strategy for every service you use.

Cool! New startup! Tumblr! Instagram! Free! No ads!

Ask yourself--how is this service making money? How is this company going to sustain itself? Every service is a company. Don't act shocked when Instagram gets acquired by Facebook and turns to ruins. Instagram didn't have a monetization strategy, and now, if you're a heavy Instagram user, you're screwed.

Only sign up for services with monetization strategies you believe in. Services that require you to pay a monthly, yearly, or one-time fee are the most sustainable. Other services rely on ads, and if you're okay with their advertising principles, go for it.

If the service looks totally free and there's no monetization strategy visible, run away. It's either going to die out or it will get snatched by a company you don't like.
